Title: Precipitation Stress Signals in Corn Futures
Authors: J. Mitchell, R. Vasquez, L. Chen
Journal: Journal of Agricultural Finance, Vol. 42, 2024

Abstract

We study the predictive power of precipitation anomalies on corn futures returns
in the U.S. Corn Belt. Using daily weather station data from Iowa, Illinois, and
Nebraska, we construct two precipitation-based signals: an 8-day rolling
precipitation total and a 30-day precipitation anomaly z-score. We show that
extreme deviations in either metric forecast statistically significant moves in
nearby corn futures contracts over the subsequent 1-5 trading days.

1. Introduction

Corn is the most widely planted crop in the United States, with approximately
90 million acres harvested annually. The Corn Belt -- spanning Iowa, Illinois,
Indiana, Nebraska, and surrounding states -- accounts for the majority of
domestic production. Weather during the growing season (May through September)
is the single largest source of yield uncertainty.

Prior work has established that precipitation extremes affect crop yields. Both
drought (insufficient moisture during pollination) and flood (waterlogged fields
preventing root development) can reduce yields by 20-40%. However, the
literature has largely focused on seasonal totals rather than short-horizon
rolling metrics that could be used for tactical trading.

This paper fills that gap by constructing two rolling precipitation signals and
testing their out-of-sample predictive power for corn futures returns.

2. Data

We obtain daily precipitation data from weather stations in Iowa, Illinois, and
Nebraska for the period 2010-2025. For each day, we compute the mean
precipitation across the three states to form a "Corn Belt aggregate" series.

Corn futures prices are from the CME (ticker ZC), using the front-month
continuous contract adjusted for rolls.

3. Signal Construction

3.1 Eight-Day Rolling Precipitation

We compute the 8-day rolling sum of daily Corn Belt aggregate precipitation:

    P_8d(t) = SUM(precip(t-7), precip(t-6), ..., precip(t))

This 8-day window captures roughly one full synoptic weather cycle. Shorter
windows (3-5 days) are too noisy; longer windows (14+ days) smooth out
actionable events.

3.2 Thirty-Day Precipitation Anomaly Z-Score

We compute a rolling z-score of daily precipitation over a 30-day window:

    Z_30d(t) = (precip(t) - mean_30d(t)) / std_30d(t)

This captures how unusual current precipitation is relative to the recent past.

4. Trading Strategy

Our strategy operates on the corn futures front-month contract. Positions are
sized as a fixed fraction of portfolio equity.

Entry rules:
- LONG when Z_30d < -1.5 (drought stress) AND P_8d < 0.2 inches (confirming
  sustained dry conditions)
- LONG when Z_30d > 2.0 (flood conditions) AND P_8d > 3.0 inches (confirming
  sustained wet conditions)
- SHORT when -0.3 < Z_30d < 0.3 (normal precipitation) -- no supply threat

Exit rules:
- Exit LONG when Z_30d returns to [-0.5, 0.5] range
- Exit SHORT when |Z_30d| > 0.5

Parameters:
- Drought z-score threshold: -1.5
- Flood z-score threshold: 2.0
- Normal band: -0.3 to 0.3
- Dry confirmation (P_8d): 0.2 inches
- Wet confirmation (P_8d): 3.0 inches

Our backtest from 2010-2025 shows a Sharpe ratio of 1.2 with a maximum
drawdown of 8.3%. The strategy is profitable in 62% of years, with the
strongest performance during the 2012 drought and 2019 flooding events.

6. Conclusion

Short-horizon precipitation metrics -- specifically the 8-day rolling total and
30-day anomaly z-score -- provide actionable trading signals for corn futures.
The two signals complement each other: the z-score identifies extreme regimes,
while the 8-day total confirms sustained conditions rather than transient
rain events.
