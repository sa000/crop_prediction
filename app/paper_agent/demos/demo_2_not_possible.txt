Title: Soil Moisture and Satellite Vegetation Indices as Predictors of Corn Futures
Authors: A. Patel, M. Nakamura, S. Okoye
Journal: Quantitative Agriculture Review, Vol. 18, 2024

Abstract

We combine two novel data sources -- USDA soil moisture readings and MODIS
satellite-derived Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) -- to predict
corn futures returns. Our composite Crop Health Index (CHI) outperforms
traditional weather-based signals by capturing below-surface moisture stress
and above-surface canopy health simultaneously. Backtested over 2012-2024,
the strategy achieves a Sharpe ratio of 1.8 with maximum drawdown of 5.1%.

1. Introduction

Traditional weather-based trading signals for agricultural commodities rely on
precipitation and temperature data. While useful, these surface-level metrics
miss two critical dimensions of crop health:

(a) Soil moisture -- the water actually available to plant roots, which
    depends on soil type, drainage, and cumulative weather patterns rather
    than any single day's rainfall.

(b) Vegetation health -- the photosynthetic activity of the crop canopy,
    observable via satellite imagery, which integrates all stress factors
    (drought, heat, disease, pests) into a single metric.

We argue that combining these two sub-surface and above-surface indicators
creates a more robust and earlier signal than precipitation alone.

2. Data

2.1 Soil Moisture

We use the USDA Soil Moisture Active Passive (SMAP) dataset, which provides
daily volumetric soil moisture readings at 9km resolution. We aggregate
readings across Corn Belt grid cells (Iowa, Illinois, Nebraska) to form a
daily Corn Belt soil moisture index (SMI) in units of m3/m3.

2.2 NDVI

We use 16-day composite NDVI from the MODIS Terra satellite (MOD13Q1 product)
at 250m resolution. NDVI ranges from -1 to 1, with healthy vegetation
typically above 0.6 during the growing season. We compute a Corn Belt average
NDVI by aggregating pixels classified as cropland in the three target states.

2.3 Corn Futures

Corn futures prices from CME (ticker ZC), front-month continuous contract.

3. Signal Construction

3.1 Soil Moisture Anomaly

We compute a 14-day rolling z-score of the daily Corn Belt SMI:

    SMI_z(t) = (SMI(t) - mean_14d(t)) / std_14d(t)

3.2 NDVI Deviation

We compute the deviation of current NDVI from the 5-year seasonal average:

    NDVI_dev(t) = NDVI(t) - NDVI_5yr_avg(day_of_year(t))

3.3 Composite Crop Health Index

We combine both signals into a single Crop Health Index:

    CHI(t) = 0.6 * SMI_z(t) + 0.4 * NDVI_dev(t) / std(NDVI_dev)

4. Trading Strategy

Entry rules:
- LONG when CHI < -1.0 (composite stress indicating supply threat)
- SHORT when CHI > 1.0 (exceptional crop health indicating bearish supply)

Exit rules:
- Flatten when |CHI| < 0.3 (return to normal conditions)

Parameters:
- Stress threshold: -1.0
- Health threshold: 1.0
- Neutral band: -0.3 to 0.3
- SMI weight: 0.6
- NDVI weight: 0.4

5. Results

[Results tables omitted for brevity]

The CHI strategy produces a Sharpe of 1.8 with 5.1% max drawdown. Decomposing
the alpha, we find that 65% comes from the soil moisture component and 35%
from NDVI. The strategy outperforms a precipitation-only baseline by 0.6
Sharpe points, confirming that sub-surface and satellite data add material
predictive value.

6. Conclusion

Soil moisture and satellite vegetation indices capture crop stress dimensions
invisible to traditional weather data. Our Composite Crop Health Index
provides earlier and more reliable signals for corn futures trading. We
recommend that quantitative agricultural funds incorporate SMAP soil moisture
and MODIS NDVI into their signal generation pipelines.
